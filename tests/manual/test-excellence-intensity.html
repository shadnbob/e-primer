📚 Academic Excellence Example

According to Dr. Sarah Johnson's 2024 peer-reviewed study published in Nature Climate Change, global temperature anomalies appear to be accelerating beyond previous projections. The research, based on 150,000 data points collected across 50 monitoring stations, suggests that current climate models might need substantial revision.

However, it's worth considering alternative interpretations of this data. Johnson herself notes in the methodology section that "correlation does not necessarily imply causation," and several factors could influence these observations. In my opinion, while the evidence seems compelling, more research is needed before drawing definitive conclusions.

What do you think about the implications of these findings? Building on Johnson's work, Dr. Michael Chen from MIT (2024) found similar patterns using an independent dataset, though he acknowledged limitations in arctic coverage. Both perspectives have merit, and the scientific community continues to debate the exact mechanisms involved.

This example demonstrates excellent scientific writing with clear attribution, nuanced language, transparency about limitations, and constructive discourse.

🚨 Problematic Writing with Intensity Levels

Intensity Level 1 (Mild)

The proposal seems reasonable and might work. It appears to address most concerns. The approach generally makes sense and usually produces good results. Critics arguably have some valid points that possibly deserve consideration.

Intensity Level 2 (Moderate)

This is clearly the best solution. Obviously, everyone can see the benefits. The evidence surely proves our point. Critics are definitely wrong about this. We must never compromise on these principles.

Intensity Level 3 (Severe)

This is absolutely, unquestionably the only solution! Anyone who disagrees is completely and utterly wrong! The situation is a total disaster and will definitely destroy everything! These evil people are literally murdering our future! It's undeniably certain that we face complete doom!

Notice how the intensity increases from subtle bias (dotted underlines) to moderate problems (solid lines) to severe manipulation (thick lines with warning indicators).

🎭 Subject Portrayal Examples

Negative Portrayal

The corrupt politician's disastrous policies have created a complete catastrophe. This evil scheme by the villainous corporation shows their immoral intentions. The shameful failure of leadership is utterly disgraceful.

Positive Portrayal

The heroic whistleblower's brilliant revelations saved countless lives. This visionary leader's revolutionary approach is absolutely groundbreaking. The noble champion of justice achieved a magnificent victory.

Subject portrayal is indicated by colored shadows - red for negative framing, green for positive framing.

🔄 Mixed Example: Problems and Excellence Together

Climate change is absolutely real and anyone who denies it is completely wrong. However, according to the IPCC's 2023 comprehensive report, the situation appears more nuanced than often portrayed. While some claim it's a total hoax (which the evidence clearly refutes), the data suggests varying regional impacts that merit careful analysis.

Dr. James Hansen's groundbreaking 1988 testimony to Congress might have oversimplified certain aspects, though his core predictions have proven remarkably accurate. In my view, based on 40 years of accumulated evidence, human activity likely contributes significantly to observed warming trends, but natural variability also plays a role.

What's your perspective on balancing immediate action with continued research? The debate sometimes becomes unnecessarily polarized, with both extreme positions ("crisis" vs "hoax") obscuring the nuanced reality that most climate scientists actually describe.

Real-world writing often contains both excellent practices and problematic patterns. The extension helps identify both.

🧪 Technical Test Patterns

Testing specific patterns for debugging:

  • Attribution: Smith et al. (2024) concluded that...
  • Nuance: This might indicate, possibly suggests, appears to show...
  • Transparency: In my opinion, I believe, limitations include...
  • Discourse: What do you think? Building on that idea...
  • Evidence: Based on 500 participants, 73% reported...
